Natural deduction

Reasoning and proof

Natural deduction

What is it?

  • Truth tables provide a way of definitively testing the validity of arguments
    • But they don't really display the reasoning that the argument involves.
  • Natural deduction is an alternate way to demonstrate the validity of arguments.
    • It does so in a way that more closely matches the process of human reasoning.
    • It involves a series of steps, each of which is justified by a proof rule.

Natural deduction

Proofs of arguments

  • In a valid argument, the premises say at least as much as the conclusion.
    • Another way to say this is that the premises include the conclusion within them.
  • A proof is a demonstration that the premises include the conclusion.
  • Through a series of steps, we pull out the conclusion from the premises.
  • A proof consists of:
    • Each of the premises,
    • A series of steps with justifications,
    • The conclusion

Natural deduction

How does it work?

  • Each step in a proof involves a method of reasoning
    • Rules of implication
    • Rules of replacement
  • Deduction is path independent.
    • The steps can be taken in any order.
    • As long as each step is accurate, the result will be the same.

Rules of implication

Why do they work?

  • Rules of implication are just mini-valid arguments.
  • Thus, they are truth preserving.
  • If your proof already includes all the premises, then the conclusion follows necessarily.
  • So, the conclusion comes for free.

Rules of replacement

Why do they work?

  • Rules of replacement are just an equivalence between propositions.
  • Thus, they show sameness of meaning.
  • If your proof includes one of the propositions, then the other proposition has the same truth value.
  • So, it comes for free.
  • Since proofs are path independent, there is no harm in repeating meanings over again.

Constructing proofs

Logic and art

Proofs in propositional logic

  • A proof is a demonstration that a conclusion follows from some given propositions.
  • We start with the premises.
  • Each step adds a new line to the proof, following the rules of propositional logic.
  • The last line of the proof is the conclusion of the argument.
  • But as long as we follow the rules, we can take any path we choose.
  • We want our proofs to stay somewhat short, so it's nice to pick an efficient path from the premises to the conclusion.
  • So, constructing a proof involves a bit of art in addition to skill with the logic.
    • As with most things, the key to constructing efficient proofs is practice.

Example proof #1

1 (S ∙ K) ⊃ R
2 K / S ⊃ R
3 ∼(S ∙ K) ∨ R Impl. 1
4 (∼S ∨ ∼K) ∨ R DeMorgan's 3
5 (∼K ∨ ∼S) ∨ R Comm. 4
6 ∼K ∨ (∼S ∨ R) Assoc. 5
7 ∼∼K DN 2
8 ∼S ∨ R DS 6,7
9 S ⊃ R Impl. 8
