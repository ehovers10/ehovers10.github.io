Induction

ampliative reasoning

Erik Hoversten

Rutgers university: LRP, fall 2014

November 10, 2014

Kinds of reasoning

Deduction and induction

Measuring the support relation

Deduction

  • conclusion necessarily follows
    • Categorical logic
    • Propositional logic

Induction

  • conclusion probably follows
    • Analogical reasoning
    • Generalization from examples
    • Statistical reasoning
    • Causal reasoning
    • Default reasoning

From premises to conclusion

Deduction

  • conclusion necessarily follows
    • Premises say at least as much as the conclusion
    • Premises include the conclusion

Induction

  • conclusion probably follows
    • conclusion might be false, even if premises are true.
    • conclusion goes beyond the premises
    • there's a jump in reasoning from the premises to the conclusion.
    • sometimes known as ampliative reasoning

Application to real life

Deduction

  • provides for certainty in the conclusion
    • useful when the standards of truth are very high
  • limited in what we can genuinely deduce from the premises
    • validity is an on/off concept
    • there are no levels of validity

Induction

  • provides less certainty in the conclusion
    • a good inference may still lead to falsehood
  • can be applied quickly, so useful when a rapid response is needed.
  • Can come in levels of stronger or weaker inference

Analogical reasoning

comparison between things

Analogy

  • Reasoning by analogy is a basic form of human reasoning.
    • We can't directly observe all things in the world.
    • But we know that different things can share properties in common
    • If we know about the nature of one thing, we can often infer something about another thing.
  • But not all such comparisons are good ones
    • Things can also share properties in common only accidentally.
    • In this case, comparing the two is not likely to lead to true beliefs.
  • Thus, we need tools for determining when an instance of analogical reasoning is good.

Analogical inference

the basic structure

  • All reasoning by analogy involves:
    • Two or more things being compared.
    • A set of properties that the things are known to share in common.
    • The proposed shared property:
      • This is known to be possessed by one of the objects
      • We infer that it is also possessed by the other objects.
  • Since it doesn't follow necessarily that the proposed property is shared by all the things, this inference is ampliative.

Analogical inference

example structure

  • Entity A has properties a, b, c, and z.
  • Entity B has properties a, b, c
  • Therefore, entity B probably has property z, too.
  • Notice that we have two major elements in the inference:
    • The entities, which we call the analogues.
    • The properties, which we call the similarities.
  • The argument doesn't explicitly mention them, but we also need:
    • The properties that the analogues don't share in common, which we call the dissimilarity.

Ways to assess the analogy

  • Relevance of the similarities
  • Number of the similarities
  • Nature and degree of the dissimilarity
  • Number of primary analogues
  • Diversity among the primary analogues
  • Specificity of the conclusion.

Relevance of the similarities

  • The properties that things share in common are often shared for a reason.
    • This is usually because the things belong to a particular type, all members of which have that property.
  • But many things share properties only accidentally
  • So, we want to make sure that the properties our analogy points to are ones that suggest the things belong to the same type.
  • This depends on what proposed property the analogy applies.
    • We say that the similarities must be relevant to the proposed property.

Relevance of the similarities

I know it when I see it

  • There is no explicit definition of relevance.
  • There are many different factors that can make two properties relevant to eachother.
  • And relevance can come in degrees
  • This is one of the things that makes induction less rigorous than deduction.

Number of similarities

  • If two things share many properties in common, then it is less likely that the similarity is accidental
  • So, the greater number of similarities you can point to, the stronger the analogy becomes.
  • But be careful!
    • Quantity is not a substitute for quality
    • If all the properties listed are irrelevant to the conclusion, then the number doesn't matter.

Nature and degree of disanalogy

  • If you want to make two things seem very similar, it's easy to pick and choose only the properties they share.
  • But the two things will also have a number of differences between them.
  • If the differences are severe, they may outweigh the similarities.
  • Alternatively, if the differences are only minor, this fact can strengthen the overall analogy.

Number of primary analogues

  • Any two things share lots of properties in common
    • Trees and the US government both have branches.
  • So comparing only two things makes it easy to point to accidental similarities they share.
  • We can improve the chance that the similarities are good ones by showing that lots of things share those properties in common.

Diversity among the primary analogues

  • Just because lots of things share a set of properties doesn't mean that they belong to the same type
  • It's possible that there is some fluke reason that they all seem to be simiar
  • The chance of a fluke is increased if all the analogues closely related.
  • So, we can improve the chance that the similarity is genuine by finding a diverse set of analogues that share all the properties in common.

Specificity of the conclusion

  • Properties can be specified in different ways
    • I have brown hair, but I also have straightish brown hair of medium length
    • Lots of people have brown hair, but much fewer have brown hair just like mine.
  • The more specific we make a property, the less likely it is that two things will share it in common.
