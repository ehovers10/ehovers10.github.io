overview

My primary research interests are in philosophy of language and cognitive science. More specifically, I'm interested in formal models of the influence of context on natural language interpretation. My dissertation explores the importance of speculation and dispute in inquiry, and I argue in favor of a model of discourse dynamics that gives the notion of speculative posits pride of place alongside conversational moves such as assertions and questions. I first examine this idea from a historical perspective and point to three classic models of inquiry (due, respectively, to William James, J. S. Mill, and C. S. Peirce) in which speculation plays a central role. I then develop a deeply collaborative model of discourse dynamics in which the semantic value boundaries of words used in discourse are set via a collective effort of the participants in the discourse. Formally, my proposal is couched within the update semantics tradition, and I motivate the formalism by applying it to two existing issues in the philosophy of language and linguistics: the proper interpretation of contrastive topic and the debate between contextualism and relativism.
